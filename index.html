Amsterdam's Power Grid Is Full!

What Does That Mean for You?

When the grid is full, Amsterdam’s energy future stalls. That means higher energy prices, delayed sustainability goals, and an increase in disruptive construction across the city.

Grid congestion and consequences explained.

The situation at a glance
Amsterdam is expanding and reinforcing its power grid — but it also needs to become smarter, to flatten energy demand and reduce the impact on urban space. Read more in this summary of Amsterdam's energy network .
Meeting electricity demand through 2030 will require annual grid investment to increase by approximately 50% by 2030 from today’s USD 400 billion. Read more in this grid analysis report .

Understand the issue
What is grid congestion?

The electricity grid in Amsterdam — the cables, transformers, and substations that carry power to homes and businesses — was built for a different era. As demand grows (electric cars, heat pumps, solar panels feeding energy back) and more renewable energy is added, the grid is running out of capacity to handle the load.

When the grid is "congested," grid operator Liander cannot guarantee that new connections or upgrades can be processed. This creates waiting lists that can last years.

- more information about grid congestion.

How does this affect my energy bill?

Congestion forces energy suppliers to manage supply and demand in more expensive ways — costs that are ultimately passed on to consumers. It also slows the rollout of cheaper, local renewable energy, keeping the city more dependent on expensive imported fossil fuels.

Households in congested areas may also face higher grid tariffs as operators invest in emergency upgrades.

Why does this slow down the energy transition?

Solar panels, heat pumps, and EV chargers all require grid connections. When the grid is full, new sustainable installations are put on hold — sometimes for 3–7 years. This directly delays Amsterdam's climate goals and means residents who want to make sustainable choices simply cannot.

Businesses face the same problem: companies wanting to install large solar arrays or switch to electric logistics are blocked, limiting the city's broader decarbonisation.

Who is responsible and what is being done?

Liander is the regional grid operator responsible for Amsterdam's distribution network. They are investing in new substations and cable upgrades, but construction takes time and faces its own bottlenecks (permits, materials, labour).

The Municipality of Amsterdam and the national government are working on policy solutions, including demand flexibility programmes, energy hubs, and revised planning rules. However, progress is slow relative to the urgency.
